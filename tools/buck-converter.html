The model

Across the on-time \(t_{ON}\) the inductor sees \(v_{IN} - v_{OUT}\) and its current ramps up; across the off-time \(t_{OFF}\) it sees \(-v_{OUT}\) and ramps down into the output. Volt-second balance sets the duty cycle, and the on-time ramp sets the current ripple:

$$D \equiv \frac{t_{ON}}{t_{SW}} = \frac{v_{OUT}}{v_{IN}}$$

$$\begin{aligned} \Delta i_L &= t_{ON}\!\left(\frac{v_{IN} - v_{OUT}}{L_X}\right) \\ &= \frac{(v_{IN} - v_{OUT})\, D}{f_{SW}\, L_X} \end{aligned}$$

The capacitor absorbs the triangular ripple current \(i_L - i_O\). It charges for exactly half a period — from one zero-crossing of that triangle to the next — collecting \(\Delta Q = \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{t_{SW}}{2}\cdot\tfrac{\Delta i_L}{2} = \Delta i_L / (8 f_{SW})\). Dividing by \(C_O\), and then substituting \(\Delta i_L\), gives the ripple in two useful forms:

$$\Delta v_{OUT} = \frac{\Delta i_L}{8\, f_{SW}\, C_O} = \frac{v_{OUT}\,(1-D)}{8\, L_X\, C_O\, f_{SW}^{2}}$$

The expanded form is the one worth internalizing. Output ripple is independent of load current, is set by the \(L_X C_O\) product, and falls with the square of switching frequency — doubling \(f_{SW}\) cuts \(\Delta v_{OUT}\) by 4×, which is why frequency is the strongest lever in the whole design and why it trades directly against switching loss in a real converter.

As a design habit, a ripple ratio \(\Delta i_L / i_O\) of roughly 20–40 % is the usual starting point — small enough for reasonable RMS losses, large enough to keep the inductor physically small.

What it ignores

Ideal components means: no conduction or switching losses, no inductor DCR or core loss, no capacitor ESR/ESL, no dead time, and instantaneous switching. The most visible omission is capacitor ESR, which in real converters often dominates the output ripple (adding roughly \(\Delta i_L \cdot R_{ESR}\)) and turns the parabolic segments into something closer to the current triangle itself. Efficiency is exactly 1 here, so \(D\) is also the ideal conversion ratio — a real converter runs slightly higher duty to cover its losses.
